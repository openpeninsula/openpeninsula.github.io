Open Peninsula
The questions →

The council says the records that govern parking, traffic and air quality around the O2 can't be found. A box of them may have been destroyed in a flooded basement in 2009.

The O2, which its operator bills as the world's busiest live entertainment venue, draws around 10 million visitors a year, next to one of London's largest new neighbourhoods, with thousands of homes built and still rising. The parking, traffic and air pollution that comes with crowds that size was never meant to be left to chance. It was supposed to be controlled and monitored, on the public record. We asked the council to show us. This was the reply, June 2026:

We understand that a box containing a significant amount of information relating to the Dome project may have been destroyed following flooding in the basement in 2009. A number of records from other sites were also affected by this incident.

“The documents you have requested cannot be located and are therefore not held.”

Royal Borough of Greenwich, response to records request EIR-7259, 11 June 2026

A flooded basement was the council's first answer, and we report it as given. But when we asked it to look again, the account changed: four of the seven documents it had called not held were produced on its public register, and it confirmed it had kept the signed 2004 agreement and every decision notice all along. The files were recoverable after all.

The records it released make the problem worse. One of the seven is still genuinely missing, another it answered with a single page, and on a third, the file that governs the station every visitor changes at, it still will not say whether it holds the records at all. So we read what it finally produced alongside what already survived: the planning files, the developer's own transport reports, the letters from TfL and the Mayor's office. The deeper we looked, the wider the gap between what the controls require and what is happening.

What we found

We rebuilt the O2's travel plan ourselves. Its public record stops after 2012.

The council first recorded it as "not held"; we reconstructed it from a TfL case study, industry coverage and an academic paper, and a copy later reached the council's own register too. It ran with monitoring to 2012, with further reviews set for 2017, 2022 and 2027, none of which appears on the public record.

The legal cap is 2,000. Current records point higher.

The most recent planning condition caps AEG parking at 2,000 spaces, yet the developer's own 2025 travel plan records 2,200, and a council study counts over 2,500 bays.

The air monitor at the traffic hotspot lost power for most of a year.

The Silvertown air-quality monitor, by the tunnel portal and the O2's event car park, captured only 21% of valid data in the tunnel's opening year. The key local pollution reading is basically unusable.

Legally bound to discourage driving, promoting parking instead.

The Outlet's Section 106 legally secures public transport ahead of car use, plus a parking strategy "designed to discourage" driving. Yet the O2 promotes free and discounted parking. We raised it. The pages were reordered to push driving down. The O2 told us nothing needed changing.

Residents go nearly car-free; the O2 doesn't.

New homes are allowed 0.1 parking spaces each; offices and shops, none. The O2 keeps 2,000, on some of the borough's most transit-accessible land, a level the Mayor's office found "significantly exceeds" London Plan standards.

The free car park nearby, left out of the count.

In 2014 TfL warned the council the retail-park car park was "free to be used for… attending events at the O2", and asked for a management condition. None was imposed. Event parking spills somewhere the O2 never has to price, monitor or count.

The bus station is tied to housing, not the crowds it carries.

By 2019 TfL said North Greenwich "no longer meets capacity requirements". Yet the upgrade that relieves it is triggered by home-building, not the O2's 10 million visitors, and the developer now wants to push that trigger from 3,000 homes to 6,000.

A car park kept for a decade, on an air-quality test that isn't there.

To keep the 723-space decked car park standing ten more years, the council was assured its air quality had been "tested and approved". The document behind that claim says the real assessment would come later. It has never turned up.

The council's first answer is set out at the top of this page: the documents could not be located, and a box of Dome records may have been destroyed after a basement flood in 2009. When we asked it to look again, that answer did not survive. Four of the seven were produced on the council's public register. The O2 Travel Plan and its monitoring reviews were uploaded there on 1 July 2026, after we asked, where the record had shown nothing. And the council confirmed it had held the signed 2004 agreement and every decision notice permanently. What its first response called lost, a second look produced.

One of the four is the O2 Travel Plan, which we had already rebuilt from outside the council: a TfL case study, transport-industry coverage and an academic paper. Together they show a flagship, 98 targets, a 20-year monitoring programme, a London Transport Award won jointly by the council and AEG, and a TfL verdict that it was good practice. It worked at first: by 2007 to 2009, around three-quarters of visitors arrived by non-car modes and 87 of the 98 targets were met.

What is harder to see is everything after. AEG produced a first review in 2010 and a second in December 2012, by which point it had moved from surveying a handful of events a year to surveying every arena event. That second review set the next ones for July 2017, 2022 and 2027. Whether any were carried out, and where they are, does not appear on the public record. The trail runs in detail to 2012, then goes quiet.

And the records the council did release describe a control regime that drifted from the start. The 2004 car park plan was built to deter driving: charges set deliberately high to keep commuters out, with a discount kept only for genuine Waterfront visitors. The same site now advertises free and discounted parking. The bus strategy was built around a rapid-transit line, the Greenwich Waterfront Transit, that the developer told the council in 2011 had been abandoned, leaving a bus-capacity gap it accepted it would have to make up.

The response is also still not complete. One of the seven is genuinely unproduced: the arena's sustainability monitoring report, which the council now says covered only construction and no longer needs monitoring. For the highways file it released a single decision notice, not the drawings and letter that notice names. And for the North Greenwich station interchange, the record that sets what the bus and tube interchange every visitor passes through was meant to be, it has not said whether it holds the documents at all. Asked, it pointed to the current masterplan. That is the interchange TfL now says "no longer meets capacity".

So the 2009 flood cannot be the whole explanation. Four of the seven surfaced the moment the record was properly searched, the agreement that binds all of it was on the register the entire time, and the travel plan was monitored and written about years afterwards. A flood may account for some old originals. It cannot account for documents produced years later, or for files recovered on a second search.

It is not a dead letter, either. On its own website today, the O2 tells drivers its parking tariffs follow "a travel plan agreement in place with the local council", one of the documents the council first said it could not produce.

That is why these files matter. They are not old paperwork; they are the control system meant to show whether the parking, travel and air-quality obligations across the O2 and the Peninsula were being kept to.

So we asked how the council satisfies itself those obligations are being kept. It said they are monitored "through the established monitoring processes," but named no process and produced no monitoring report.

How the car-lite promise plays out on the ground

The Peninsula was consented as a car-lite place, with parking deliberately constrained because this is some of the borough's most transit-accessible land, beside North Greenwich station. On the Government's own evidence to Parliament, the scale of building here was the most the transport network could take, and was "only achievable" because the crowds were assumed to arrive by public transport rather than by car. Here is how that has played out around the O2.

A 2,000-space cap. The records point higher.

An enforceable condition from October 2024 caps AEG parking at 2,000 spaces. The limit is not arbitrary: parking here was constrained from the original consent "to restrict the number of traffic movements", yet every later figure we found is higher:

A "temporary" car park, kept for another ten years

The same October 2024 permission carried a second decision: a 723-space decked car park could stand for up to another ten years, to 2034. The deck was only ever meant to be temporary, built to replace O2 parking during the Silvertown Tunnel works and approved on the explicit basis that "a separate planning permission is required for its retention". The tunnel opened in April 2025. The deck did not come down: a structure put up as temporary became a decade-long fixture. And the reason given to keep it, that its air quality had already been "tested and approved", is contradicted by the very document it cites.

That justification does not hold up. Asked in August 2024 whether any air-quality assessment had been done for the ten-year extension, the applicant said a "further technical assessment… hasn't been undertaken", because the car parks had already been "tested and approved" in the masterplan's Environmental Statement. But that Statement says the opposite: for the new car park "there was insufficient information for a quantitative assessment", and "a detailed assessment… will be undertaken at the reserved matters stage". The document relied on as having tested the car park is the same one that said the test would come later, and whether it was ever carried out is unanswered.

Residents near car-free, the O2 at 2,000

This is one of London's largest residential schemes: the masterplan consents up to 12,678 new homes, a community the developer's own marketing calls "homes for 20,000", with thousands already built and more still rising. And the same scheme that squeezed those new homes from 0.7 parking spaces each in 2004 to 0.25 in 2015 to 0.1 by 2020, and held offices and retail car-free, re-provides 2,000 O2 event spaces. The Mayor's office found the O2 parking "significantly exceeds… London Plan standards" and required "robust justification". TfL accepted it only because a legal agreement binds the operator to re-provide the spaces to 2062:

“TfL reluctantly concedes that… this departure from policy T6(L) must be accepted to enable the delivery of the Masterplan…”Transport for London, in the council's Masterplan committee report, 2021

Measured from the mapped footprints, almost all of that ground is at grade, not stacked in decks, and it sits directly on top of a Jubilee line terminus, some of the borough's most transit-accessible land, the very place planning policy holds everyone else to near car-free. Newer London event venues are now planned the other way round: Tottenham Hotspur Stadium, opened in 2019, has no public car parking at all and an event-day controlled parking zone.

Scale: the 2015 masterplan (15/0716/O) consents up to 12,678 C3 dwellings (Development Specification / Condition 05); the developer's own marketing describes the Peninsula as "homes for 20,000" (greenwichpeninsula.co.uk). It is a London Plan Opportunity Area being built out in phases to around 2037. Residential parking ratios: 0.7 spaces per home (2004), 0.25 (2015), "up to 0.1 spaces per dwelling unit per plot" (20/2000/NM, 2020), with offices and retail car-free in the transport work (WSP Transport Assessment, Outline Part, July 2019, Table 7.2). Driving was meant to be discouraged. It's being promoted

The O2 and its outlet were approved on a charging regime. Their operator signed a binding commitment to run a parking strategy "designed to discourage use of private cars" which, in the agreement's own words, "for the avoidance of doubt will include car parking charges", alongside a travel plan that put public transport ahead of the private car and a duty to monitor and report car use. The concern is not new. In 2012, when the outlet was approved, TfL told the council its parking "should be reduced and controlled to discourage car use", already more than twice the London Plan maximum. And the charging strategy was real: TfL's own 2010 case study recorded the O2's parking as "dis-incentivised" and "a premium price", sold by pre-booking for events, the deliberate opposite of a free-parking offer.

That was AEG's own position too, for years. Its 2012 travel-plan monitoring report states that "driving to The O2 is discouraged in favour of sustainable modes of transport", that "such high parking ticket prices act to deter drivers", and commits to a pricing strategy "across all AEG car parks that deter drivers". Its 2015 travel plan calls the top parking rate a "punitive charge" and says rates are "likely to increase year on year to continue to discourage car travel to The O2". In its own documents, the operator treated paid parking as the deterrent. The 2025 partnership turns that on its head.

The O2's own sustainability page tells fans "the majority of our carbon footprint is generated by fan travel", and urges them to take public transport, "from tube and bus to ferry and cable car". One of those options, the cable car, was recorded as having almost no regular commuters on TfL's figures, and is used mostly by tourists for the view.

None of this rests on a poor green record. The O2 is England's first "Greener Arena", and says it has "surpassed its… greenhouse gas emissions targets every year since 2010". But the wins it counts are the ones it controls: energy, lighting, waste. The one it admits is the majority, fan travel, stays outside those targets, outside the monitoring its consent required, and pulled the wrong way by the parking the venue promotes.

The customer journey does the opposite:

  • Four hours' free parking when you spend £35 at the Outlet
  • A second four hours free for cinema-goers
  • Event parking sold as a ready-to-book add-on, "up to 30%" off
  • Weekly, monthly and even annual parking passes, sold to "save money"

In May 2025 the O2 went further, naming JustPark its "official parking partner" in a first-of-its-kind, multi-year deal. AEG's own vice-president put the aim plainly:

“…make it easier than ever for attendees to drive, park and enjoy their visit.”Nathan Kosky, VP Europe, AEG, on the JustPark partnership (May 2025)

JustPark's own research, cited in the announcement, found parking hassle can deter up to 87% of fans from driving to an event; the deal is built to remove exactly that deterrent. The commitment the operator signed was to discourage driving, with charges as the lever; the partnership, and the discounts and passes around it, pull that lever the other way.

And in the weeks after we raised it, the long-stable travel pages were reordered to move driving down the page, while AEG told us it saw no changes as necessary.

The pull toward driving also cuts against the council's own current Transport Strategy 2022–2032, which asks key tourism destinations (it names the O2) to steer visitors to public transport, and says "sustainable and active travel options" should be "prioritised above car travel to and from major events".

The roads are already busy before events

This is already a busy residential, school, station and venue district before event traffic is added. The developer's own December 2024 surveys counted roughly 3,900 person trips in one morning peak around the new homes and school. Closer to the O2, Department for Transport counts on the road serving its car parks show weekday car traffic rising from roughly 4,900 to 7,500 vehicles between 2008 and 2019, a rise the counts attribute to the area's general build-out rather than to the O2 alone.

Those counts show the ordinary baseline, not the weekend and event-night peaks. On one O2 event day, Boxing Day 2024, a Transport for London freedom-of-information response recorded 108 penalty charge notices in a single day around the neighbouring North Greenwich station car park, where a driver reported being trapped in congestion with no spaces free and no signage warning that the car park was full. It was one day, and not a typical one, but it is what no spare capacity looks like on the kind of day the area exists to host.

The pressure is significant enough that Transport for London plans its own operations around it. Its Board papers keep the Blackwall Tunnel running northbound through the night so O2 crowds can cross the river home, and choose tunnel-closure dates to avoid "major O2 area events"; clearing cars from the venue has taken "over 90 minutes", enough that TfL fitted a bespoke automated signal system at the car-park exit, one of only about 50 such critical locations in London. The council records the same in its own Transport Strategy 2022–2032, which names the O2 for "sharp spikes in demand", "traffic congestion" and more "contested kerb spaces" on the Peninsula.

A designated air-quality corner, near the limit and barely monitored

The whole borough is an Air Quality Management Area, and TfL's own Silvertown baseline puts the Peninsula's approach road, the A102 past the tunnel portal, at the highest excess delay of any route in its study area, already near the legal nitrogen-dioxide limit, "sensitive to traffic changes", and, on TfL's own forecast, still short of World Health Organisation air-quality guidelines even by 2030. Yet at the exact corner where the tunnel, the O2 car parks and venue traffic converge, the effect has barely been measured: TfL's reference monitor at the portal lost power for most of the tunnel's opening year and captured just 21% of valid data, nearly four-fifths of the year missing, too little to use. A reliable post-opening picture was not expected until around mid-2026, and as of July 2026 none had been published: the most recent monitoring report remained the three-month post-opening report of July 2025.

The same corridor exceeds the stricter WHO guideline at its other end. Of the borough's eleven air-quality monitors, the council's own strategy singles out the one at the Woolwich flyover, beside the free retail-park car park the planning record says is free to be used for O2 events:

“The monitoring station at the Woolwich flyover is the only site which is regularly exceeding the WHO limits.”Royal Borough of Greenwich Transport Strategy 2022–2032

The corridor this venue cluster feeds is bracketed by air-quality problems at both ends, while the traffic the car-lite plan is meant to discourage goes onto it uncounted. (The A102 is a major tunnel approach, so its through and freight traffic dominates these readings; the point is that the venue traffic on top is neither separately measured nor managed.)

At the station, the promised changes haven't happened

The car park beside the station is the largest of any Underground station within Greater London. At about 500 spaces, only Epping, out in Essex at the far end of the Central line, has more:

And it was never meant to be this big, or even to survive. Two car parks beside the station were meant to go: a commuter car park the 2019 modelling assumed "removed by 2021 in all scenarios", and a customer car park a 2006 obligation bound TfL to surrender. Both still operate in 2026. TfL has now answered what the planning file left open: its eastern lot, Car Park 2 (418 spaces), is leased to the GLA and used "to contribute towards the contractual provision of car parking spaces for the O2". That makes it O2 parking on TfL land, beside the site's 2,000-space cap rather than inside it, while the 509-space commuter car park alongside is separate. TfL also says it has "not surrendered any part of our car parks", that the land is locked until December 2028 by a Knight Dragon option to build the bus station, and that the parking income goes into its general network, rather than the interchange the land is reserved for. The land's status is unsettled even for its owner: it sits in public ownership, yet asked whether the railway byelaws that normally govern a station car park still apply here, TfL replied that it holds no such record.

The bus station shows the same gap between plan and delivery. The new bus station and cycle network were the masterplan's own answer to its biggest transport impact, yet the upgrade is tied to home-building rather than the O2's crowds, and the developer now proposes to slip its trigger from 3,000 homes to 6,000, into the 2030s, even as TfL calls North Greenwich a station that "no longer meets capacity requirements". TfL's internal emails, released under EIR-0773-2627, show the case for that deferral was assessed on 502 morning-commuter trips from the new homes, with event demand left out of the capacity test.

That deferral did not go unopposed. Both TfL and the council's own highway officers objected to it, TfL warning of a shortfall in bus-standing space and crowding at the stops before later mitigation satisfied it. A March 2026 briefing to the council leader went further still: the existing station might be kept as it is rather than rebuilt, subject to a feasibility study.

That leaves out most of who uses the station. TfL's own figures make North Greenwich the fourth-busiest bus station in London, around 39,500 passengers a day, and the O2 alone draws over 10 million visitors a year, against a masterplan of about 12,678 homes. And the newest venues rely on it wholesale: Secret Cinema and Troubadour, beside the O2, are by their own transport statements explicitly car-free, their audiences put entirely onto the buses and tube, with their own joint assessment estimating up to a 10% cut in Jubilee capacity in the half hour after a show. On a near-isolated peninsula that TfL itself calls "an unusually high degree of dependence on a single Underground line", those are the crowds a resident-only trigger leaves untested.

The neighbouring venues' car-free basis and cumulative transport impact are from their own transport statements: the Secret Cinema Transport Statement (i-Transport, April 2026) sets the development as car-free and, with the adjacent Troubadour Theatre (planning ref 25/3391/F, also car-free), models a worst-case combined audience of 4,500 producing up to a 10% reduction in Jubilee line capacity in the half hour after a show, a worst case the assessment notes is unlikely to occur in practice. North Greenwich as the fourth-busiest bus station in London, about 39,500 passengers a day, is TfL pre-application advice GRNW19/275 (30 April 2019); the single-Underground-line dependence is TfL's Cable Car Need and Business Case (2011).

The low-car record leans on a single Underground line, the Jubilee. TfL's own modelling, in its O2 case study, estimates car use roughly doubles, to 40 to 55 percent of visitors, when the line is closed. It was closed across a full event weekend in January 2026, leaving O2 visitors and a nearby football crowd with a handful of replacement buses. When the line is down, far more of the crowd drives, onto the same roads and into the same parking the venue promotes.

The questions they have not answered

None of this is an accusation. It is a set of questions the responsible bodies have not answered, drawn entirely from their own records:

  • Which documents now govern the O2's parking, travel plan and monitoring? The council first said it could not produce the originals, produced four when pressed, and still has not said which now apply. (Royal Borough of Greenwich)
  • What holds parking to the 2,000 cap when the operator's own website advertises 2,500? Nothing on the record reconciles the enforceable limit with the spaces actually marketed. (Royal Borough of Greenwich)
  • Was the free-parking promotion ever tested against the duty to discourage car use? The Section 106 requires a strategy "designed to discourage use of private cars"; the discounts, passes and JustPark deal pull the other way. (Royal Borough of Greenwich / The O2)
  • Do the roughly 500 station car-park spaces sit inside or outside the cap, and what is that land's legal status? TfL holds "no such record" of the byelaws governing it. (Transport for London)
  • When will the event-evening parking controls the residential streets need be required? The retail-park overflow and drop-off spillover remain unmonitored and unmanaged. (Royal Borough of Greenwich / TfL)
  • Where is the air-quality assessment for keeping the decked car park ten years? It was said to be "tested and approved", but the document relied on says the test would come later. (Royal Borough of Greenwich)

Two of these are now formal records requests: EIR-7259 to the council, the request that produced the flooded-basement reply, and EIR-0773-2627 to TfL, answered in July 2026. The fuller set has been put to the council, TfL and the O2 directly. The records either exist or they do not; either way, the answers are theirs to give.

The way forward

None of this is a case against the O2, or against the events people come for. They will keep coming; the only real question is how they get here. The Peninsula was built on a straightforward bargain: that a place this dense beside a Tube station could work because people could arrive without a car. Keeping that bargain is not a punishment; it is the plan:

1

Produce the records

Release the documents meant to govern the O2's parking, travel plan and monitoring.

2

Establish and enforce the 2,000 cap

The O2 already operates above it. Hold AEG to the limit, and ratchet it down toward the car-lite standard the rest of the Peninsula meets.

3

Deliver the capacity

Build the public-transport capacity the Peninsula was promised, so arriving without a car is genuinely easy.

The keystone

That last step is the keystone. The point was never to force people out of cars with nowhere else to go, which would only move the same traffic onto the same streets and the same overstretched line; it was to make arriving without a car genuinely easy, so the pressure to drive eases of its own accord. Done that way, it serves everyone at once: today's residents, the venue, and the new homes and public realm this land was always meant to deliver. The pieces were all promised once; they can still be delivered.

The parking cap, and what is operated

The O2's parking was capped to control traffic. Over time the ceiling crept upward, the live permissions stopped agreeing on it, and the operated figure settled above the line.

  • The cap has teeth. The current enforceable condition, 24/1874/F Condition 3 (October 2024), sets "a total of 2,000 car parking spaces across the Greenwich Peninsula", requires decked spaces to be "partially or fully closed" if it is exceeded, and requires the operator to produce evidence of the number "within one month of receipt of any written request". The council does not have to estimate the figure; it can require it.
  • The ceiling crept, and the permissions now disagree. The 2015 Development Specification allowed "up to 2,000"; a 2020 amendment (20/2000/NM) widened it to a "2,000 to 2,150" range, and a 2023 one (23/3796/NM) to "up to 2,150". The two live permissions carry different caps: 2,150 under 15/0716/O, 2,000 under 19/2733/O (Condition 58) and 24/1874/F.
  • What is actually operated has barely moved in twenty years. The 2004 consent allowed 2,200 spaces "to restrict the number of traffic movements" (Public Accounts Committee, HC 409, 2005); the O2's contractor then installed exactly "2,200 spaces across four car parks" for the 2007 opening (Parkeon, 19 April 2007); and the developer's 2025 travel plan still records 2,200, its own consultant putting it at "over 2,100". The operated figure has tracked that original 2,200 throughout, even as the enforceable cap was set lower, at 2,000, and the most recent records (shown above) run higher still.
  • It is run and audited by a contractor. The car parks are operated by a third party, CSP (the Combined Services Provider), appointed by AEG in 2006 with "cash and audit services" among its listed duties, so the true, audited number sits with the operator and AEG. (Consumer event-parking booking and pricing is a separate arrangement again, through JustPark and The O2's own pages.)

So the operated number sits above both the 2,000 cap and the older 2,150 parameter, and Condition 3 is a direct, inspectable way for the council to establish the real one: it exists, it is audited, and it can be required within a month.

The infrastructure that was deferred, and the traffic already here

The infrastructure meant to carry the growth has been pushed back, while the surveyed trips are already high. This is the fuller record behind "The roads are already busy before events" and "At the station" above.

  • The deed of variation 24/4133/I106 (validated 5 Dec 2024) proposes to move the bus station and cycle facilities triggers from 3,000 to 6,000 homes, and the first primary school from 2,500 to 5,000 homes; about 2,572 homes have been delivered. The developer said this followed "an assessment undertaken by our consultant team and confirmed by TfL". TfL's internal emails on that assessment, released under EIR-0773-2627 (July 2026), show it rested on the new homes' own trips: the consultant identified the AM peak as the highest demand, an additional 502 trips for 6,000 homes, and TfL agreed there were "no capacity issues at 6,000 homes based on the most recent available data". TfL's covering reply confirms its analysis "is done on am and pm peak hour capacity so would not include events". In the same emails TfL notes the Millennium Way overflow stands are kept "for resilience purposes, especially on o2 event nights", so the event demand was acknowledged but sat outside the capacity test that justified the deferral. The disclosed demand tables cover a single typical day with no event scenario, and the Millennium Way overflow stands that absorb O2 event nights were held outside the regular-service capacity counted as headroom; the consultant's own note adds that how often those event stands are actually used was not examined. Correspondence and officer briefings on the same deed, released under a separate request on 24/4133/I106, show the deferral did not pass unopposed: TfL's consultation response of 26 March 2025 objected to the application, citing a "risk of a shortfall in bus standing space" and "passenger crowding at bus stops", before withdrawing the objection on 8 June 2026 after interim works and alternative bus-standing provisions were added; the council's own highways officers also objected to the cycle-facilities deferral; and the Chief Planning Officer's briefing to the council leader of 5 March 2026 recorded the bus-station trigger moving from 3,000 to 6,000 homes with an option to retain the existing station subject to a feasibility study, and the first primary school moving from 2,500 to 5,000 homes.
  • The Gateway Junction Improvements programme (23/1843/I106) places the new bus station and cycleway works around January 2031 and the main junction handover to the council in January 2037.
  • The new bus station was the masterplan's own answer to its biggest transport impact: the 2015 Environmental Statement (15/0716/O) rated the scheme's effect on the bus network "moderate negative" and brought it to "negligible" only by relying on the new station and extra services. Yet TfL's 2014 options review already called the station "approaching capacity", and its April 2019 pre-application advice (GRNW19/275) described North Greenwich as "the 4th busiest Bus Station in London", with about 39,500 passengers a day, that "no longer meets capacity requirements". The mitigation that made the impact acceptable on paper is the same one now proposed to slip to 6,000 homes.
  • On the neighbouring IKEA scheme, about £260,000 secured to improve access to local Tube and rail stations was, a 2021 freedom-of-information response showed, mostly spent instead on street lighting, so transport money committed to the area did not all reach its stated purpose.
  • The rest of that scheme's transport obligations are, on the council's own record, only partly delivered. Answering a freedom-of-information request (EIR-7325, July 2026), the council confirmed it holds no baseline or annual travel-plan monitoring surveys for the store — the Year 0, then years 1, 3 and 5 surveys the approved Travel Plan set out and the S106 required be carried out — stating "no further surveys have been submitted to the Council", and "no review, correspondence or decision record" for the point covering a shortfall against the customer car-driver target. Of the two contributions paid to the council in January 2018, the £500,000 secured for extra buses and two relocated bus stops is recorded as unspent — £534,150 with indexation, "pending transfer to TfL" — more than eight years on; the £750,000 Travel Plan Fund is recorded as £736,097 spent (signage and pedestrian/cycle access, the Peartree Way junction, a temporary Charlton–Greenwich cycleway and traffic orders), with £65,128 left. And on non-retail use of the shared car park — the informal O2 park-and-ride described elsewhere on this page — the council returned "nil": no monitoring report, enforcement record or correspondence held since the store opened. (The council answers only for what it holds; non-submission is not the same as the surveys never being carried out.)
  • The Strategic Travel Plan Monitoring Report (Arup, May 2025; surveys 4 December 2024) records, in the morning peak: 1,573 residential person trips, 814 commercial, and 1,511 for St Mary Magdalene school: roughly 3,900 combined, before O2 events, temporary events, the cable car, the pier, parkrun, visitors and future homes. The same report logs resident concerns about lighting, crossings and cyclist–pedestrian separation "especially along the Thames Path".
  • Over a longer run, the Department for Transport's own roadside counts on the road serving the O2's car parks show weekday car traffic there rising about 52% between 2008 and 2019 (roughly 4,900 to 7,500 vehicles across a 12-hour day), as the surrounding area built out. Honest limits: these are single weekday counts (each a Thursday in late spring), so they understate weekend and event-night peaks; the latest is 2019, before the pandemic and the tunnel; the rise tracks general build-out around the road, not the O2 specifically; and they measure traffic passing on the road, not a turnstile count of the car park itself. (The counts do peak at 5–6pm, but an evening peak is the norm on most roads and is not itself evidence of leisure or event use.)
  • What one of those peaks looks like in practice is on the public record. On Boxing Day 2024, during an O2 event, TfL's own freedom-of-information response (FOI-3502-2425, 18 February 2025) records 108 penalty charge notices issued in a single day around the North Greenwich station car park (these may include red-route penalties on the adjacent A102, not only car-park fines), where the requester described being trapped in event congestion, with no spaces free and no signage warning the car park was full. It is one day, and not a typical one, but it shows what no spare capacity looks like on the kind of day the area exists to host: the O2's own help pages state up to 2,000 cars may leave its car parks at once on an event day, and AEG puts the venue at around ten million visitors a year. TfL retains the car-park CCTV for only 30 days, so the footage was overwritten before it could be requested.

The school figure is worth holding onto: around 1,511 morning trips, including children, use the same routes that event crowds disperse onto.

Transport for London's own papers repeatedly plan major roadworks around the O2's event car traffic. A June 2009 TfL Board paper, placing barriers on a new link road by the Blackwall Tunnel, noted that otherwise "a large number of vehicles may utilise this new link to access the nearby O2 arena". The June 2010 paper on the £70m Blackwall Tunnel refurbishment kept traffic running northbound overnight because the arrangement "enables patrons of the O2 to travel southbound across the river to attend evening events and return northbound after events", warning that restricting it "would create significant local traffic problems across a wide area of south London", and it chose weekend closure dates to avoid "major O2 area events". The venue's event car traffic is significant enough to shape how TfL runs a major river crossing, yet it is the same traffic the car-lite plan and the discourage-driving duty are meant to hold down. The problem is long-standing: a 2010 TfL Board paper records that clearance times for vehicles leaving the O2 had reached "over 90 minutes", and that TfL installed a bespoke automated signal system at the car-park exit in 2009 to manage it, one of only around 50 such critical locations in London.

The council says the same in its own words: its Transport Strategy 2022–2032 (para 3.23) names the O2 for event "spikes in demand" that bring "traffic congestion", "greater demands for public transport" and more "contested kerb spaces" on the Peninsula.

The station car parks and the surrender obligation

The fuller record behind "At the station" above: two car parks assumed to be gone or shrinking, both still operating, and the public-land questions around them.

  • Both "gone or shrinking" assumptions are documented, and both went unmet. The commuter car park's removal "by 2021 in all scenarios" is in the WSP Transport Assessment (Outline Part, July 2019, para 8.2.4), which kept the O2 parking "retained or re-provided"; because that car park is still open in 2026, the assessment baseline deleted commuter trips that still exist. The customer car park's surrender is in Mayor's Question 2006/0465-1: under 2002 agreements TfL was bound to surrender half before the Dome re-opened and the rest later, yet it still operates, and TfL's own 2021 programme (Mayor's Question 2021/0104) now treats surplus station car parks as housing sites.
  • This has been unresolved for a long time. In January 2007 the station car park was a 913-space London Underground car park that, local press reported, had just "lost some 500 spaces to the Anschutz Company, which runs The O2, for a temporary car park", and was itself expected to "eventually close" (News Shopper, 9 January 2007). So roughly 500 station spaces were handed to the venue's operator nearly two decades ago, and how today's spaces are counted against the O2's 2,000-space cap traces back to that transfer.
  • That customer car park is publicly branded as a "North Greenwich Station Car Park (TfL)", but it is managed by a private operator (Saba) on behalf of TfL's property company (TTL Properties / Places for London), which took the car parks over from NCP in January 2023. Saba's own public listing gives 509 spaces. When TfL was asked (FOI-0703-2526, 2025) it pointed to that management arrangement. TfL has since confirmed that the separate eastern lot beside it, Car Park 2 (418 spaces), is leased to the GLA and used for the O2's contractual parking; what stays open is the terms on which this 509-space commuter lot operates.
  • Asked under freedom-of-information rules (FOI-0703-2526, internal review June 2025) whether the railway byelaws that normally cover a station car park still apply to this land, or had been revoked, TfL's review panel said it had been "unable to identify" any such record and that, "on the balance of probabilities… the information is not held". On the ordinary principle that byelaws stay in force until formally revoked, that absence is itself telling: the land's legal status is undocumented by its owner, even as it is run as ordinary private parking. The TfL property register (Property Asset Register, par.tfl.gov.uk) records the car-park land as held by Places for London on a lease (titles TGL539603 and TGL539628), over land in public ownership; the wider Peninsula freehold on the same register is held by GLA Land and Property Ltd, the legacy of the site's public assembly for the Dome.
  • A 2025 permission, 25/2707/F (granted 20 October 2025), reduces coach parking "from 67 to 25" to "facilitate the release of land for… plot 20.02"; TfL recorded a concern about coaches or lorries then "parking up on the council's road network".

Older planning figures vary; the useful, answerable question is the car park's current occupancy on weekdays, weekends and event days. (For how its ~500 spaces compare across the network, see the chart above.)

The retail-park car park, and event parking spillover

The venue is required to price, manage and monitor its own parking to discourage event-night driving; a free car park the planning record says is used for those same events sits about a mile away, with no equivalent control. This is the full record behind "The free car park the count leaves out" above.

  • The leisure park's own rules exist, but don't catch it. Its published policy restricts the spaces to "visitors to the leisure park only", five hours free, customers who "may be asked to provide proof of purchase". In practice it is policed by number-plate cameras enforcing a five-hour maximum stay, which a gig comfortably fits inside, while the "leisure visitors only" rule rests on a discretionary proof-of-purchase check that, residents report, is not made even when the car park fills on event nights and the shops and cinema are closing. The control that exists limits how long you stay, not whether you are a customer, and that lever sits with the operator (Legal & General) as much as the council.
  • The developers have known for years. The 2013 transport assessment that won the IKEA store its permission records that the car park's old management scheme was by then "no longer enforced", so "in effect, there are no limits on who can park and for how long", and concedes the car park "is being used as an informal park and ride for some commuters and visitors into central London". The applicant's answer was that management "will be considered if long stay parking becomes an issue".
  • The management plan that followed targets shoppers, not events. Secured through the site's S106 (Clause 6.1) and in force: number-plate cameras, a five-hour maximum stay, a £70 penalty for overstaying, tightened in 2021 so a penalty issues from the first overstay. But its stated objective is to "discourage commuter parking", it runs only "during the hours in which the stores trade", and five hours covers a gig, so the non-customer event parking TfL asked it to address is exactly what it leaves untouched.
  • The developer's own May 2025 reports say as much in writing. Its transport assessment records the car park open until 11pm, and midnight on Fridays and Saturdays, with the five-hour ANPR limit, and that "visitors would be able to use the car park within the opening hours and for durations of under five hours, as per the existing situation". Its parking management plan is blunter: the limit exists "to enable customers to complete a full shop and linked trip", and "there will be no charge for parking less than five hours", so the only control is duration, never whether you are a customer or why you came.
  • TfL asked for event controls; IKEA refused. When TfL asked, in 2021, that controls "extend through the night and at weekends" to stop parking by non-customers and residents, the applicant replied it could not deter visitor parking "without greater levels of control" such as in-store validation, that this "would be detrimental to IKEA customer experience", and that "if the arrangements are considered too onerous, IKEA will not accept the proposals".
  • The council's own highways team predicted it. When the east Greenwich IKEA, marketed as the chain's "most sustainable" store, sought five hours' free parking in 2019, the council's highways officers warned that "events at the O2… are likely to attract motorists to also park at the site", and that long stays could cause "congestion… affecting the highway". The objection was withdrawn and the five-hour limit approved.
  • And the pressure is set to grow. In 2025 the council approved 1,251 homes on the IKEA car park itself (a "car-free" estate whose residents are barred from the retail-park spaces and from local street permits), cutting the retail park's own parking from 1,066 to 856. The applicant conceded a "risk of parking overspill onto nearby residential streets", and the Charlton Society objected that parking "will spill into surrounding areas like Charlton… necessitating new Controlled Parking Zones".

The duty the O2 carries to price, manage and monitor its parking has no counterpart at the free car park a mile away: every control that would catch event parking there has been asked for and either declined, or left to a check that residents say isn't made.
