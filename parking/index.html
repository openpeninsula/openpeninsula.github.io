Greenwich Peninsula · A residents' page on parking, traffic & air quality

Greenwich Peninsula was designed to be car-lite. A lot of what's happening now points the other way — and several of the documents meant to keep it in check can't be located.

The Peninsula sits on top of a Jubilee line station, so its parking was deliberately kept low: the O2's spaces capped, new homes built nearly car-free, a travel plan meant to put people off driving, and new buses and cycle routes promised to carry the growth. These were planning conditions and legal obligations, not loose promises.

This page lays out where the plan and the reality seem to part company. Every point is a discrepancy and an open question, not an accusation — each one asks the council, TfL or the O2 to show the records that explain the current position.

What we're asking for

This isn't a call to ban cars, stop events, or halt new homes. It is a request for four basic checks:

  • Which approved documents currently govern the O2's parking, travel plan, air-quality monitoring and car-use reporting?
  • How is compliance with the 2,000-space cap and the discourage-driving strategy actually being checked?
  • Was the later detailed air-quality assessment for the bus terminal and car park ever carried out, and where is it recorded?
  • If key documents cannot be located, how can the combined impact on the area be assessed today?

Greenwich Peninsula is exactly the kind of place where car-lite planning makes sense. The question is why that same logic appears not to be applied as transparently to major venue parking, free-parking promotions and event-related car activity.

We're setting out discrepancies and open questions, not allegations.

What doesn't add up

There's more parking than the cap allows.

The latest condition caps O2 parking at 2,000 spaces. The developer's own 2025 report records 2,200, and a council study counts over 2,500 bays.

The air-quality sign-off points to a missing assessment.

Keeping the 723-space decked car park was justified as already "tested and approved" in the Masterplan Environmental Statement. But that same ES said a detailed air-quality assessment of the car park would come later.

Residents go nearly car-free; the O2 doesn't.

New homes get 0.1 parking spaces each. The O2 keeps 2,000 — which the Mayor's office found "significantly exceeds" London Plan standards.

Driving was meant to be discouraged. It's being promoted.

The O2's transport case relied on charging to put people off driving. Today a promotion offers free parking to shoppers who spend over a threshold.

The transport upgrades were pushed back.

The new bus station and cycle routes were due by 3,000 homes. That's been moved to 6,000, with main junction works now set for 2031–2037.

The roads are already busy — before events.

The developer's own December 2024 survey counted about 3,900 trips in one morning peak, before any O2 event, the cable car, the pier or future homes.

The monitoring documents can't be found.

The council can't locate the approved travel plan, parking plan, bus strategy or air-quality monitoring for the O2 — its reply to records request EIR-7259.

AWhat the Peninsula was promised

The Greenwich Peninsula was consented as a car-lite place. The masterplan and its planning obligations lean on walking, cycling, the Jubilee line, buses and river services, with parking deliberately constrained because the area sits at the highest public-transport accessibility level, beside North Greenwich station.

For the O2

A firm ceiling on parking

Alongside the parking limits, the 2004 legal agreement secured an air-quality monitoring station and an arena sustainability framework with independent reporting, and a bus service improvements strategy. A new, relocated bus station and cycle network were to be delivered as the homes arrived — "before the occupation of 3,000 dwellings".

BWhat's actually happening

More parking than the cap allows

The most recent enforceable condition (October 2024) caps AEG parking at 2,000 spaces, and requires decked spaces to be "partially or fully closed" if that is exceeded. It also gives the council a direct way to check: AEG must supply evidence of the operated number "within one month of receipt of any written request". The figure is long-standing: the 2015 masterplan's own Environmental Statement assessed the O2's parking at 2,000 spaces, to be re-provided as a permanent multi-storey car park.

Yet the developer's own May 2025 travel plan records that the O2 "currently operates four car parks with a total of 2,200 car parking spaces", with a further 500-space station car park alongside. The applicant's own April 2026 transport statement puts the four O2 car parks at 2,226. A council retail study from November 2025 describes "over 2,500 bays available".

This is the one point that is both fully documented and directly testable. The question is simple: what evidence does the council hold that AEG parking does not exceed 2,000, and has the closure mechanism ever been used?

A "temporary" car park, kept for another ten years

That 2,000-space cap comes from a permission granted in October 2024 — one that also kept a 723-space decked car park standing for up to another ten years, to 2034. The structure was built only as temporary, to replace O2 parking during the Silvertown Tunnel works, on the explicit basis that "a separate planning permission is required for its retention" once the tunnel was finished. Rather than coming down when the tunnel opened in April 2025, it was kept — the developer's stated aim being to hold this decked capacity so surface car parks can be cleared for housing, all within the O2's standing 2,000-space obligation. Either way, a structure put up as temporary is becoming a decade-long fixture on land at the highest public-transport access, while the permanent car parks meant to replace it remain pending on other plots.

Residents near car-free, the O2 at 2,000

The same scheme that holds new homes to 0.1 spaces each, and makes offices and retail car-free, re-provides 2,000 O2 event spaces. Before the 2019 masterplan was approved, TfL's own position was that the O2 and station parking "should be allocated for disabled persons parking provision only so that the scheme is policy compliant". The Mayor's office, reviewing the same application, found the O2 parking "significantly exceeds… London Plan standards and robust justification for this is required", and that residential parking "must be reconsidered".

Driving was meant to be discouraged — it's being promoted

The transport acceptability of the O2 and its outlet rested on a charging regime: a strategy "designed to discourage use of private cars", including parking charges, and a travel plan that relied on a "punitive charge". A current retail promotion advertises free parking for shoppers spending over a threshold, and the event-ticketing journey presents parking discounts as a convenience, with no equivalent public-transport prompt.

The promotion is the point in question, not whether car parks should exist: a free-parking incentive sits against the charging strategy the transport case was built on.

The transport upgrades were pushed back

The new bus station and cycle facilities were to arrive by 3,000 homes. A 2024 deed of variation moved that trigger to 6,000 homes, and the first primary school from 2,500 to 5,000 homes; about 2,572 homes have been delivered so far. The junction and bus works that justify the intensification are programmed well into the future — the new bus station and cycleway around January 2031, and the main junction handed to the council in January 2037.

The new bus station was not a nice-to-have. It was the masterplan's own answer to its biggest transport impact: the 2015 Environmental Statement rated the scheme's effect on the bus network "moderate negative", and brought that down to "negligible" only by relying on the new bus station and extra services — a station TfL had already described as "approaching capacity" back in 2014. So the mitigation that made the bus impact acceptable on paper is the same one that has been pushed back.

And where money is committed, it does not always reach its stated purpose. On the neighbouring IKEA scheme, about £260,000 earmarked to improve access to local Tube and rail stations was — a 2021 freedom-of-information response showed — mostly spent instead on street lighting.

The roads are already busy, before events

The developer's own monitoring, from surveys in December 2024, records in a single morning peak: about 1,573 residential person trips, 814 commercial, and 1,511 for the school next door — roughly 3,900 combined, before any O2 event, temporary event, cable car, pier, parkrun or future homes are added. The same report notes resident concerns about lighting, crossings and the separation between cyclists and pedestrians "especially along the Thames Path".

Two station car parks that were meant to shrink or go

When the masterplan was assessed in 2019, the O2's own car parks were to be "retained or re-provided" on a "lift and shift" basis. The separate commuter car park beside North Greenwich station was treated differently: in the transport consultant's own words, it was "to be removed by 2021 in all scenarios". It is still open in 2026 — so the modelling that judged the scheme's traffic and air quality assumed away commuter car trips that, in practice, still happen.

There is also a longer-standing obligation. In 2006, answering a question at the London Assembly, TfL said it was contractually bound to surrender the North Greenwich customer car park to support the Peninsula — "half… ahead of the Dome's re-opening", and the remainder later. That car park is still operating too. It is publicly branded as a "North Greenwich Station Car Park (TfL)", yet it is run by a private operator on behalf of TfL's property company. That raises a fair question of control and counting: who controls this land, on what terms, and are its roughly 500 spaces counted inside or outside the O2's 2,000-space cap? The useful answer is not a disputed bay number but the current occupancy — on weekdays, weekends and event days.

Coach parking cut to release a venue plot

A 2025 permission reduces coach parking "from 67 to 25" spaces, to "facilitate the release of land for… plot 20.02" — the plot now proposed for Secret Cinema. TfL recorded a concern that this could leave "coaches or lorries parking up on the council's road network".

The official numbers may miss what residents see on the ground

The formal parking figures do not necessarily capture all event-related car activity. For well over fifteen years — in online discussions dating back to 2008, years before the 2015 masterplan set its car-lite standard — event-goers have openly described the same workaround: park free at IKEA, B&Q or a nearby retail park and walk or bus the rest of the way, precisely because it sits outside the O2's own charges and count. Others pay for a cinema ticket, or spend at the Outlet, to unlock free or discounted parking. Residents, in turn, report people walking back through residential streets late at night, and recurring pickup and drop-off congestion around East Parkside and Edmund Halley Way — an issue the developer has itself acknowledged and looked at moving elsewhere.

The leisure park's owner already has rules for exactly this — they just don't catch it. The car park's published policy restricts the spaces to "visitors to the leisure park only", with five hours free and customers who "may be asked to provide proof of purchase". In practice it is policed by number-plate cameras enforcing a five-hour maximum stay — which a gig comfortably fits inside — while the "leisure visitors only" rule rests on a discretionary proof-of-purchase check that, residents report, is not made even when the car park fills on event nights and the shops and cinema are closing. The enforcement that exists limits how long you stay, not whether you are a customer. That lever sits with the operator (Legal & General) as much as the council.

None of this is new, and the developers have known it for years. The 2013 transport assessment submitted to win the IKEA store its planning permission records that the car park's old management scheme — three hours free, attendants logging number plates and ticketing overstays — was by then "no longer enforced", so "in effect, there are no limits on who can park and for how long". It goes further, conceding that the car park "is being used as an informal park and ride for some commuters and visitors into central London". The applicant's answer was that management "will be considered if long stay parking becomes an issue" — and the permission that followed secured no such control: its only parking condition simply requires the spaces to be retained, not policed.

There is a documented thread to this, too. When the east Greenwich IKEA — marketed as the chain's "most sustainable" store — sought up to five hours' free parking in 2019, the council's own highways officers warned that "events at the O2… are likely to attract motorists to also park at the site", and that long stays could cause "congestion and possibly subsequent queues… affecting the highway". The objection was later withdrawn and the five-hour limit approved. The spillover its highways team anticipated is now exactly what event-goers openly describe.

The venue itself is held to the opposite approach. The O2's own transport case, as reported to the Mayor in 2012, rests on discouraging car trips: its operator told planners it was "adopting a car parking pricing strategy to discourage car trips", that car use "would be monitored and reported as part of the overall O2 travel planning commitments", and that it would enter "a legal agreement with the local planning authority in relation to car parking charges and a car parking management plan" — expressly to "minimise clashes with O2 event night demand for car parking". So the venue is required to price, manage and monitor its own parking to hold event-night driving down, under a legal agreement; a free car park the same authority says is used for those same events sits a mile and a half away, with no equivalent requirement on anyone, and the management condition TfL asked for there in 2014 was never imposed.

And the pressure looks set to grow. In 2025 the council approved 1,251 homes on the IKEA car park itself — a "car-free" estate whose residents are barred from the retail-park spaces and from local street permits — cutting the retail park's own parking from 1,066 to 856. The applicant's own assessment conceded a "risk of parking overspill onto nearby residential streets", and the agreed mitigation is a legal agreement barring the new residents from ever applying for a parking permit. The Charlton Society objected that, with no residents' parking, "parking will spill into surrounding areas like Charlton… necessitating new Controlled Parking Zones". Car-free on paper, the objection warns, can still mean more cars on the surrounding residential streets.

These patterns sit outside the simple question of how many official O2 spaces exist. If parking, pickup and drop-off and informal event access are spilling into residential streets, they should be monitored and managed as part of the venue cluster's real transport impact — not treated as invisible because they fall outside the formal car parks. How many event-goers park this way, and where, does not appear ever to have been measured — so the gains a car-free neighbourhood is meant to deliver are offset by visitor traffic that is neither counted nor controlled.

CThe documents that would answer this can't be found

Everything in section A — the cap, the travel plan, the parking management, the bus strategy, the air-quality and sustainability monitoring — was meant to be governed by approved documents and checked through monitoring reports. These aren't obscure files. They are the approved documents that set out how a major venue's parking, traffic and air quality were supposed to be controlled. Most of them are not currently inspectable.

The planning portal lists the submissions below as approved or decided, but the document tabs hold only the decision notice, not the approved document itself. In June 2026 a request under the Environmental Information Regulations (EIR-7259) asked the council for them:

  • Dome Car Parking Management Plan — the approved basis for O2 parking management (04/0365/SD)
  • O2 Travel Plan — the plan underpinning the discourage-driving logic (06/2805/SD)
  • Bus Service Improvement Strategy — the bus mitigation (06/2671/SD)
  • Air Quality Monitoring Station — the monitoring obligation (06/2511/SD)
  • Arena Sustainability Framework Monitoring Report — the sustainability monitoring (07/1254/SD)

The council carried out searches of its archived records and replied that "no documents matching those reference numbers could be located", and that "the documents you have requested cannot be located and are therefore not held". It offered an explanation:

We understand that a box containing a significant amount of information relating to the Dome project may have been destroyed following flooding in the basement in 2009. A number of records from other sites were also affected by this incident.

Royal Borough of Greenwich, EIR-7259 response, June 2026

That is the council's own account, and it is taken at face value here: this is about records that cannot currently be inspected, not about anything being deliberately removed. But the consequence stands either way. The documents that would show whether the parking cap, the travel plan, the charging strategy and the monitoring obligations have been kept to cannot, at present, be produced for a major venue and a parking operation of this size. The question the page asks is how that is to be remedied — which documents currently govern these obligations, and how compliance is being monitored without them.

The air-quality trail points to a missing later assessment

Air quality is the sharpest example. The 2004 agreement required an air-quality monitoring station for the O2 and the Peninsula — and that is one of the documents the council now says it cannot locate.

It matters because of how the 723-space decked car park was justified. In August 2024, the council's environmental health team asked whether any air-quality assessment had been carried out for keeping the decked car park for another ten years. The applicant replied that "a further technical assessment… hasn't been undertaken", because the O2 car parks "were assessed in the Environmental Statement which supported the Masterplan application". On that basis, it said the temporary parking was equivalent to the permanent arrangement already "tested and approved".

But the Environmental Statement says something different. Its air-quality chapter records that, for the new bus terminal and car park, "there was insufficient information for a quantitative assessment to be undertaken", and that "a detailed assessment of the proposed bus terminal and car park will be undertaken at the reserved matters stage".

So the document now being relied on as having tested the car park is the same one that said the detailed assessment would come later. The open question is simple: was that detailed assessment ever carried out, where is it recorded, and how is current air-quality monitoring being checked now that the Silvertown Tunnel is open?

DWhat we're asking the council, TfL and the O2

These are addressed to the bodies that hold the records, and framed as questions. Two requests under the Environmental Information Regulations are live: EIR-7259 to the council (which has now replied that the older documents are not held), and EIR-0773-2627 to TfL, due 3 July 2026.

For the Royal Borough of Greenwich

  • Which approved documents currently govern O2 parking, the travel plan, parking pricing and car-use monitoring — given the 02/2903/O approved documents could not be located?
  • The latest condition caps AEG parking at 2,000 with evidence available on request. What evidence does the council hold that the operated number does not exceed 2,000, how is the "over 2,500" figure reconciled, and has the closure mechanism been used?
  • Has the free-parking promotion been reviewed against the approved travel plan, the car parking strategy and the obligation to discourage car use?
  • How is the continued operation of the commuter car park — assumed removed by 2021 in the masterplan modelling — reconciled with the assessed transport and air-quality impacts?
  • The 2004 agreement secured an air-quality monitoring station for the O2 and the Peninsula. Is it operating, where is its monitoring data published, and does it yet reflect the Silvertown Tunnel that opened in April 2025?
  • As parking plots come forward for redevelopment, will the quantum be assessed against current London Plan Policy T6 (car-free the starting point) rather than simply re-provided?
  • Are event-related parking, pickup and drop-off, and pedestrian spillover through residential streets — including retail-park parking, East Parkside and Edmund Halley Way — being monitored, and what evidence is held on the scale, timing and management of these impacts?
  • Who maintains and funds the public realm and landscaped areas absorbing this spillover, and how is that cost met?

For TfL and the Mayor's office

  • What recorded assessment confirmed deferring the bus and cycle trigger from 3,000 to 6,000 homes, and was TfL formally consulted? (EIR-0773-2627, due 3 July 2026.)
  • The commuter car park beside the station was assessed as "removed by 2021 in all scenarios". Why is it still open in 2026, and was its continued operation ever reassessed for traffic and air quality?
  • What is the status of the 2006 obligation to surrender the North Greenwich customer car park — who controls the land and on what terms, and are its roughly 500 spaces counted inside or outside the O2's 2,000-space cap? What is its current occupancy on weekdays, weekends and event days?
  • The Mayor's office found the O2 parking to "significantly exceed" London Plan standards, needing "robust justification". What justification was accepted, and does it still hold now the Silvertown Tunnel is open and the bus and cycle triggers deferred?

For AEG and The O2

  • How many parking spaces are currently operated and promoted, and are former station car park spaces counted within the approved AEG maximum or treated as additional to it?
  • How do parking promotions, validation and free parking sit with the travel plan commitment to discourage car use?

Public, but not easy to inspect

The planning record is technically public. In practice, the key information is scattered across hundreds of files, with important points buried in environmental chapters, appendices, legal agreements, later amendments and monitoring reports. Some approved documents are missing from the portal entirely.

Open Peninsula exists because residents should not need to become planning archaeologists to understand how a car-lite neighbourhood is being monitored.

The parking cap, and what is operated

The O2's event and leisure parking was given a firm ceiling, but the ceiling crept upward over time, and the operated and advertised figures now sit above it.

  • The 2015 Development Specification set a control parameter of "Up to 2,000 parking spaces". The 2022 outline permission, 19/2733/O Condition 58, says "up to 2,000 AEG parking spaces". The most recent enforceable condition, 24/1874/F Condition 3 (Oct 2024), caps AEG parking at "a total of 2,000 car parking spaces across the Greenwich Peninsula", requires decked spaces to be "partially or fully closed" if exceeded, and requires evidence "within one month of receipt of any written request".
  • The parameter crept: from "Up to 2,000" (2015) to a "2000 to 2150" range (20/2000/NM, July 2020) to "up to 2,150" (23/3796/NM, 2023). The two extant permissions now carry different caps — 2,150 under 15/0716/O, 2,000 under 19/2733/O and 24/1874/F.
  • The operated figure: the Strategic Travel Plan (May 2025) records "2,200 car parking spaces" across four O2 car parks, plus a 500-space station car park; the applicant's April 2026 transport statement gives 2,226; the council's November 2025 retail study describes "over 2,500 bays available".

So the operated number appears to sit above both the 2,000 cap and the older 2,150 parameter, and Condition 3 provides a direct, inspectable way for the council to test it.

Residents near car-free, the O2 at 2,000

The contrast between how residents and the venue operator are treated is set out in the developer's own transport work and the Mayor's reports.

  • Residential parking fell from 0.7 spaces per home (2004) to 0.25 (2015) to "up to 0.1 spaces per dwelling unit per plot" (20/2000/NM, 2020); offices and retail are car-free in the transport work.
  • TfL's pre-application position (letter GRNW19/275, 30 April 2019) was that the O2 and station parking "should be allocated for disabled persons parking provision only so that the scheme is policy compliant".
  • The Mayor's Stage 1 report (GLA/5010a/01, 11 Nov 2019) found the O2 parking "significantly exceeds draft London Plan and London Plan standards and robust justification for this is required", and that residential parking "exceeds London Plan and draft London Plan standards and must be reconsidered".
  • An earlier Mayor's report on the retail outlet (2012) found its parking above the London Plan maximum and occupied to "only… half of the maximum capacity", with charges that were supposed to be "set at a level to discourage car trips… secured through a section 106 agreement".
The infrastructure that was deferred, and the traffic already here

The infrastructure meant to carry the growth has been pushed back, while the surveyed trips are already high.

  • The deed of variation 24/4133/I106 (validated 5 Dec 2024) moved the bus station and cycle facilities triggers from 3,000 to 6,000 homes, and the first primary school from 2,500 to 5,000 homes; about 2,572 homes have been delivered. The developer said this followed "an assessment undertaken by our consultant team and confirmed by TfL" — the point that EIR-0773-2627 is testing with TfL directly.
  • The Gateway Junction Improvements programme (23/1843/I106) places the new bus station and cycleway works around January 2031 and the main junction handover to the council in January 2037.
  • The Strategic Travel Plan Monitoring Report (Arup, May 2025; surveys 4 December 2024) records, in the morning peak: 1,573 residential person trips, 814 commercial, and 1,511 for St Mary Magdalene school — roughly 3,900 combined, before O2 events, temporary events, the cable car, the pier, parkrun, visitors and future homes. The same report logs resident concerns about lighting, crossings and cyclist–pedestrian separation "especially along the Thames Path".

The school figure is worth holding onto: around 1,511 morning trips, including children, use the same routes that event crowds disperse onto.

The station car parks and the surrender obligation

Two car parks beside the station were assumed to be gone or shrinking. Both are still operating.

  • The WSP Transport Assessment (Outline Part, July 2019) states at para 8.2.4 that "the existing commuter car park is to be removed by 2021 in all scenarios", distinguishing it from the O2 parking, which is "retained or re-provided". The commuter car park remains open in 2026, so the assessment baseline deleted commuter car trips that still exist.
  • In answer to Mayor's Question 2006/0465-1 (11 December 2006), TfL stated it was bound by 2002 agreements "to surrender half of the North Greenwich customer car park ahead of the Dome's re-opening" and "contractually obliged to surrender the remainder… at a later date". The customer car park still operates. TfL's own later programme (Mayor's Question 2021/0104) treats surplus station car parks as housing sites where drivers have alternatives.
  • That customer car park is publicly branded as a "North Greenwich Station Car Park (TfL)", but it is managed by a private operator (Saba) on behalf of TfL's property company (TTL Properties / Places for London). When TfL was asked (FOI-0703-2526, 2025) it pointed to that management arrangement. The open question of control and counting remains: on what terms the land now operates, and whether its roughly 500 spaces sit inside or outside the O2's 2,000-space cap.
  • A 2025 permission, 25/2707/F (granted 20 October 2025), reduces coach parking "from 67 to 25" to "facilitate the release of land for… plot 20.02"; TfL recorded a concern about coaches or lorries then "parking up on the council's road network".

No bay count is cited for the customer car park here — published figures conflict. The useful, answerable question is its current occupancy on weekdays, weekends and event days.

The missing approved documents (EIR-7259), and the policy backdrop

The records that would prove the obligations were discharged and are being monitored could not be located by the council.

Under the 02/2903/O permission, the approved documents for the Dome Car Parking Management Plan (04/0365/SD), the O2 Travel Plan (06/2805/SD), the Bus Service Improvement Strategy (06/2671/SD), the Air Quality Monitoring Station (06/2511/SD) and the Arena Sustainability Framework Monitoring Report (07/1254/SD) are not on the portal — only the decision notices are. The council searched its archives and confirmed the documents "cannot be located and are therefore not held", citing a possible loss of Dome-related records in a 2009 basement flood.

Two pieces of the current policy backdrop matter to the questions above. The Silvertown Tunnel opened on 7 April 2025, which changes the traffic and air-quality picture the older assessments assumed. And current London Plan Policy T6 makes "car-free development… the starting point", and says that where sites are redeveloped, parking "should… not be re-provided at previous levels" where that exceeds the standards — the mechanism that would ratchet parking down as the Peninsula's car parks come forward for redevelopment.
