They quietly dropped our free neighbourhood fete. Now they want a ticketed Disney show on our doorstep.

FREEFor the community

Per person

Their own footage of the fete is still on the page announcing its "pause". The event itself is gone.

For ten years, the free Urban Village Fete was one of the Peninsula's few genuinely free community events, drawing around 30,000 people a year. This year it did not happen. No mention in their newsletter. No social post. No proper announcement. Just one quiet line on their own website:

Urban Village Fete will take a pause in 2026 as we look ahead to an exciting new chapter on the Peninsula, with major cultural arrivals including Troubadour Greenwich Peninsula Theatre and Secret Cinema.

Greenwich Peninsula, official Urban Village Fete page

That "exciting new chapter" is not another free community event. It is two ticketed venues, including Secret Cinema's Pirates of the Caribbean, from £49 a ticket*, already sold out for February.

And these are marketed nationally and internationally, not to the neighbourhood: the Troubadour bills itself for "world-class productions and international live entertainment", and the same Disney show is sold UK-wide through O2 Priority, Visa, TodayTix and Disney.

The developer has not clearly announced this to residents. If you are reading it here, you may be among the first to hear the fete is gone.

The problem, in short

Free public culture out, paid visitor entertainment in.
  • The Peninsula was sold through free public culture: the Urban Village Fete, NOW Gallery, the weekend market, the Jetty, public art and the riverside.
  • Now the free fete is "paused" while two ticketed venues are presented as the Peninsula's "exciting new chapter".
Up to 2,250 people onto the riverside path.
  • The applicant's own figure: up to 2,250 people leaving Secret Cinema and Troubadour together in a single half hour.
  • They load onto Olympian Way / the Thames Path, where the walking space is about 1.5 metres in places, and no one has assessed whether it stays usable.
Sold as permanent, applied for as temporary.
  • Secret Cinema is marketed as a "new permanent home", and Troubadour as "semi-permanent".
  • But both rely on temporary "meanwhile" applications for up to ten years, on land planned for homes.
Infrastructure already over capacity.
  • TfL says North Greenwich bus station is the fourth busiest in London and "no longer meets capacity requirements".
  • It already serves a 20,000-seat O2 and a 10,000-capacity Magazine London; the two new venues would bring up to 4,500 more, with no one modelling the realistic overlap.
Close to homes, late, and loud.
  • The venues sit among homes, with a new residential tower rising about 25 metres from the cinema's facade.
  • They would run late with amplified music and special effects, on a riverside recorded at birdsong levels, where the theatre's own acoustician already predicts a breach of the noise limit.
Like the O2, but on our doorstep.
  • In planning terms this is destination-scale ticketed entertainment, not a community facility.
  • The masterplan put this kind of use by the O2 and station, not on a residential riverside.

What to ask for

Ask the council to:

  • refuse Secret Cinema, submitted without the crowd, transport and cumulative assessments it needs
  • carry out a site visit before deciding, rather than judging it from renders
  • assess Secret Cinema, the Troubadour, The O2 and other major Peninsula venues together
  • require a proper crowd safety and pedestrian capacity assessment of Olympian Way and the Thames Path
  • assess the effect on the cycle route, station, buses, taxis, drop-offs and local streets
  • secure all mitigation, including queuing, crowd dispersal, pick-up and drop-off and noise controls, by enforceable condition, not by voluntary or post-opening management plans
  • refer the application to the public Planning Board, not delegated approval

The council should also weigh the loss of public riverside amenity, late-night noise, lighting, advertising, and whether ticketed commercial venues should be treated as a community benefit while free public amenities disappear.

The Troubadour next door, and the operator's record at Canary Wharf

The operator points to its Canary Wharf theatre as proof these venues work. But Canary Wharf has a large public square at the door, and Greenwich does not.

The Troubadour next door is the bigger venue: two auditoria, up to 3,000 seats, nearly 25 metres tall, open until 1am at weekends, about 80 metres from the nearest existing homes. It would use the same riverside route as Secret Cinema, and was resolved to be granted in January 2026 but is still awaiting a final decision, its Section 106 not completed.

This is the developer's own image of the entrance.

This is their own picture of a calm afternoon. Now put the audience for two full auditoria, up to three thousand people, arriving and leaving across that 1.5-metre footpath and the cycle lane, past the loungers people use to rest.

At Canary Wharf the crowd spills out across a large open public square that absorbs it. Greenwich has no such concourse: the entrance opens straight onto Olympian Way, beside the Thames Path and National Cycle Route 1, and the nearest open space is a landscaped plaza of sculpture, trees, benches and bollards, built for sitting and slow movement, not for absorbing a theatre crowd. The comparison the operator invites makes the problem clearer, not weaker.

  • Bigger: Canary Wharf holds about 1,200; the Greenwich proposal is up to 3,000 across two auditoria.
  • Opening problems (October 2025): long queues in the rain, unfinished areas, crowding on exit routes that attendees described as unsafe, and accessibility complaints from wheelchair users.
  • That single way out is the same vulnerability these plots have, so crowd safety and step-free access should be secured by condition before approval, not deferred to a management plan after the doors open.

And it will be loud. This riverside is quiet today, at birdsong levels in the developer's own survey, yet the Troubadour's own acoustician already predicts the theatre would exceed the council's noise limit at the very plot where Secret Cinema would sit, before the cinema's own crowd and sound are added on top.

And the way it was sold does not hold up: the "85% support" was 30 of 35 responses, gathered on the developer's own channels, and the boards advertised around 500 jobs for a venue that is about 65.

The path: 1.5 metres, and the crowd it must carry

Up to 2,250 people leave the two venues in a single half hour, onto about 1.5 metres of walking space beside a two-way cycle route. Nobody assessed whether it works, and on TfL's own guidance it does not.

The Thames Path here was built as the Peninsula's "P5K" route, a five-kilometre running and walking loop for the neighbourhood, conditioned to stay public "in perpetuity", not for event crowds. When the path itself went through planning, TfL and amenity groups asked for an assessment of how many people it could carry; the developer said none was needed, because it was "a leisure route, not the primary commuter route". The one time its capacity was raised, they said it did not need checking, and now they want to empty event crowds onto it. On the applicant's own drawings the two-way cycle track takes about three metres; the walking strip beside it is about one and a half, and it is part of National Cycle Route 1, so a crowd does not only fill the path, it blocks cyclists riding straight through.

And it is the amenity, not just a route. P5K Phase 2 was a landscaping scheme: a designed public leisure space for the neighbourhood, the upper level for walking, running and cycling, the lower level for sitting and relaxing by the water. An event crowd bottlenecking onto it, and onto the seating terrace below, turns that amenity into the venues' forecourt. The loss of a designed public leisure space is a planning harm in its own right, separate from whether the path can physically carry the crowd.

It is also part of the designated Thames Path National Trail, protected by national, regional and local planning policy. Emptying an event crowd onto it at peak, so that it is not safely usable, runs against that protection (the specific policies are set out in the councillors' section below).

The developer's answer is that the path "will remain clear" and a queuing plan will keep crowds off the cycle route. That is an assurance, not an assessment: the plan is not yet written, it covers people arriving rather than the 2,250 leaving at once, and no one has measured whether the strip can hold them. And the same developer describes Olympian Way very differently when selling the homes next door, its own statement for the block now rising to the south calls it "a public footpath" and "the pedestrian only pathway", the neighbourhood's primary walking route, not an events exit.

Its own visitor guides go further, selling this very path as a place for "a leisurely stroll", "a quiet spot" and "quiet time to yourself", a traffic-free run and a cycling "safe haven" along the river, and the wider Peninsula as "tranquil, green spaces" for "well-being". That is the calm, restful character an event crowd of up to 2,250 would displace.

The applicant calls this 2,250 peak its own worst case, and "unlikely", because the two operators "will coordinate" their show times. But that coordination is not secured by any condition, the two venues share one developer and one narrow corridor, and even on the applicant's own worst case no one has assessed whether the footway works.

Why the lower path and the Design District route do not solve it

  • There is a lower riverside path, but along the venue frontage it is a separate level, divided from the upper footway by a strip of seating, loungers and planting and crossed only at two staircases. A crowd cannot pour down two stair points, and the two paths merge into one only at each end, away from the entrances, so it cannot be relied on to take the overflow.
  • On TfL's own Pedestrian Comfort Guidance, the applicant's 2,250-in-half-hour flow across the roughly 1.5-metre footway lands in the worst band, "unacceptable" for a residential street.
  • Even granting a generous 2.6 to 3 metres, more than the marked strip, it is still "very uncomfortable", worse than the guidance accepts at peak. The cycle lane is not crowd space, and the applicant assessed none of this.

And the alternative they point residents to does not solve it. Secret Cinema's resident Q&A says egress will be "through the Design District", but its own Transport Statement routes everyone via Olympian Way and never mentions it, and the Design District can only be reached along Olympian Way in the first place, the same narrow corridor. Nor is it open ground: the approach is landscaped public space, grass, trees, fixed bollards and benches, built for slow movement and rest, not a concourse a crowd of thousands could pass through without trampling it.

The station and streets, already over capacity

The same station and streets are already over capacity on an ordinary O2 night, and nobody has assessed a realistic peak day across all the venues at once.

After almost every O2 event, North Greenwich jams for the best part of an hour. It is a routine sight, filmed and posted by visitors again and again.

That is the arena on its own, about 20,000 people, and it is not the only venue, nor is the station ready:

  • TfL calls North Greenwich the fourth busiest bus station in London and says it "no longer meets capacity requirements". The new bus station meant to fix it is not built, and the developer has applied to push its trigger from 3,000 to 6,000 homes (not yet determined); the venues' crowds count toward neither threshold.
  • Magazine London, also on the Peninsula, holds up to 10,000, and its own advice tells people to come by Tube and bus. Nobody has modelled a realistic peak day: an O2 event plus a Magazine event plus the two new venues, on one station and the same few streets.
  • And the O2 is a year-round destination, not just event nights. On top of the arena, its outlet, cinema and restaurants pull a continuous daily crowd: the O2 as a whole recorded over 10 million visitors in 2024, nearly all arriving through the same single Tube and bus interchange, before either new venue is added.
  • Many skip the buses and drive, parking at the retail park by IKEA and walking in through East Parkside and the streets around it, parked cars, drop-offs and horns into the evening, none of it counted in the applicant's transport work.
  • Pick-ups and drop-offs land on East Parkside and Edmund Halley Way, the residential streets, where private hire, taxis and family cars wait and idle into the evening. The developer has tried to move this off the estate with a pick-up car park on Millennium Way, but admits to residents that there is still an issue on East Parkside. The further fixes are unfinished, geo-fencing is being added to the taxi apps and a physical barrier is only "being considered", and geo-fencing reaches app-based taxis only, not private cars or family pick-ups. Measures still in progress, on a problem the developer accepts persists, should be secured by condition, not left to keep trying after the doors open.

The masterplan: this is housing land

These plots are zoned for housing. The masterplan put venues like these by the station, not on the riverside it set aside for homes.

  • In planning terms a theatre and a cinema are "Sui Generis" uses: ticketed destination entertainment, not a library, museum or community hall. The council's own description of Secret Cinema (26/1247/F) says "Sui Generis".
  • The masterplan put uses of that kind in the centre, by the station and the O2, away from homes, and zoned the riverside plots, including these two, for housing.
  • The Mayor of London's own cultural-design guidance agrees: venues like these need central, well-connected sites with space to gather, queue and leave, and it warns a queuing crowd's noise can be a worse nuisance to neighbours than the show inside. These plots offer none of that, only the 1.5-metre path.

So this is not culture arriving in the neighbourhood. It is a commercial entertainment venue, moved off the central, well-connected ground the plan set aside for a theatre, onto a riverside the plan set aside for homes, with homes nearby and more being built right beside it.

The point is not that the Peninsula can never have venues. Knight Dragon itself previously promoted theatre, cinema and performance uses at Peninsula Place, over the North Greenwich transport interchange, in what it called "the heart of it all".

That is exactly where this kind of use belongs: beside the Tube, bus interchange and O2, with space to gather, queue and leave. Not on Lower Riverside outside homes, opening onto a 1.5-metre walking strip beside the Thames Path.

And the free culture that was here was the developer's own. Its placemaking consultant, Futurecity, appointed by Knight Dragon in 2014, describes the free NOW Gallery and the Jetty as "meanwhile activation" cultural projects, part of a "cultural DNA" strategy to establish the Peninsula "as a key cultural destination". Those free, public cultural uses are the ones now being wound down, as ticketed commercial venues arrive in their place.

The "temporary" label also matters. These are not weekend pop-ups; they are up to ten-year commercial venues. A temporary permission can sit outside the normal masterplan route for housing, infrastructure and cumulative assessment, even while creating real, long-term pressure on the same streets, paths and services.

And the venues are not really sold as temporary at all. The Troubadour is marketed to investors as a ten-year venture, in the listing's own words "semi-permanent, high-capacity venues" disrupting "the traditional real estate and theatre models", not the brief meanwhile use the planning case relies on. And Secret Cinema, the application actually being decided, is no different: the developer's own website calls it Secret Cinema's "new permanent home" and "new long-term home", and Secret Cinema's own site a "flagship home", even as the application is only for a temporary use of up to ten years. And the label travels: before settling on Greenwich, the same operator won Camden's approval, in early 2024, for a "long-term" home in a former bingo hall, then walked away from it within months. "Permanent" here is branding attached and dropped from one site to the next, not a commitment a temporary permission can rely on.

The planning case in brief, for councillors and the Greenwich Society

The issue is not whether Greenwich Peninsula can have culture or theatre. The issue is whether this specific site has been properly assessed before a decision is made.

The corridor was never assessed.The applicant's Transport Statement models up to 2,250 people leaving Secret Cinema and the Troubadour in a single half hour, routed onto Olympian Way and the Thames Path. But it contains no pedestrian comfort assessment of that corridor, where the marked walking strip is about 1.5 metres beside a two-way cycle route. The Planning Statement says the path will "remain clear / unaffected", but that is an assurance, not an assessment.

This is housing land, with no venue allocation.Plot 20 is zoned residential under the operative 2015 masterplan. The Peninsula's only theatre allocation in the 2022 Development Specification is by the Transport Interchange, and expressly excludes this plot. Instead of amending the masterplan, the venues are being brought forward as separate temporary "meanwhile" permissions for up to ten years, even while the developer markets Secret Cinema as a "new permanent home" and the Troubadour is marketed to investors as "semi-permanent".

Each venue was assessed in isolation.Two adjacent crowd-generating venues, from the same master developer, have been applied for separately. Neither properly models the other, and neither tests the realistic cumulative case with The O2, Magazine London, North Greenwich station, buses, taxis, drop-offs and local streets.

The noise baseline understates the real case.Secret Cinema's noise survey was taken on a night with "no events at The O2", against a 43 to 45 dB baseline. The realistic worst case, both venues turning out while The O2 empties, is not modelled. The Troubadour's own acoustician already predicts theatre noise exceeding the adopted limit at the Secret Cinema plot.

The supporting infrastructure is already over capacity, and its fix is being deferred.TfL's own assessment calls North Greenwich the fourth busiest bus station in London and says it "no longer meets capacity requirements". The replacement bus station has not been built. The developer has also applied to push its delivery trigger back from 3,000 to 6,000 occupied homes under S106 variation 24/4133/I106, which has not yet been determined. Event crowds generated by these venues do not count toward either dwelling trigger.

The path is a safeguarded National Trail.Olympian Way here forms part of the Thames Path National Trail. The council's adopted Core Strategy safeguards the existing riverside and Thames paths; London Plan Policy SI 16 requires public access along the waterway to be protected; and the NPPF requires development to protect and enhance public rights of way and access, including National Trails. Sending up to 2,250 people onto this route at peak, without a pedestrian comfort assessment, should be assessed against those policies before any decision is made. (National Trail status is material through these policies, not a separate statutory veto.)

These are requests for proper assessment before decision, not objections to culture or theatre.
