What residents were shown, and what is in the planning file

The image used in posters, emails, the website and press coverage is the dramatic night render: Secret Cinema lit up on the riverside. The planning file gives a fuller view of the building residents would live beside, including the daytime frontage, the raised podium, the stairs, the long switchback ramp, railings, and the side and rear elevations.

This is not a small local screening. Secret Cinema is promoting a ten-week run of Disney's Pirates of the Caribbean, with tickets advertised from £49 per person and VIP packages up to £299. Tickets have been sold through national platforms including O2 Priority, Visa, TodayTix and Disney, and February dates have already sold out.

The venue's own material describes an immersive event with a live band, stunts, on-stage special effects, loud music and crowds. It would run Monday to Sunday, around eight shows a week (the days and number subject to change), with matinee and evening performances across the ten-week season. On the applicant's indicative evening timings, doors open about 6.15pm, the show runs to about 9.45pm, the bar takes last orders at 10.15pm and the audience is out by 11pm, while on some Thursday, Friday and Saturday nights a "Post-Show Event" keeps the site open later still. The Thursday to Saturday evening shows are recommended for over-18s, with under-16s not admitted, while other performances are recommended for over-12s, with under-7s not admitted. So arrival and departure peaks recur day after day, with audiences leaving late into the evening. The applicant treats this frequency as a point in its favour, arguing the audience is spread over "an (up to) seven day operational period each week", so it avoids the traffic and noise of a one-off festival or concert. For residents, that spread is the burden rather than the relief: the same peaks return most days, for ten weeks at a time, under a permission of up to ten years.

Food, drink and bar spaces are part of the ticketed experience, behind the entry price, rather than a new public place residents can use without buying a ticket. The venue's own visitor information gives the address as Olympian Way and sends visitors on the short walk to and from North Greenwich station, routing its audience onto the same path and transport network residents already use.

The Troubadour comparison

The operator points to its Canary Wharf theatre as evidence that this type of venue can work. That comparison is useful, but the setting is very different.

The Troubadour proposed beside Secret Cinema is the larger venue: two auditoria, up to 3,000 seats, with a fire-safety venue capacity of up to 3,500 once the public bar and restaurant are counted, nearly 25 metres tall, and open until midnight Sunday to Thursday and 1am on Friday and Saturday, seven days a week, with the public bar and restaurant open throughout (and later still under occasional Temporary Event Notices). It would use the same riverside route as Secret Cinema. It was resolved to be granted in January 2026, but is still awaiting a final decision while its Section 106 is completed.

At Canary Wharf, people can gather outside the venue in a large public square. At Greenwich, the proposed audience would arrive and leave via Olympian Way / the Thames Path, beside National Cycle Route 1. The nearest open space is landscaped with sculpture, trees, benches and bollards, designed for sitting and slow movement rather than absorbing a large theatre crowd.

This is why the two venues need to be assessed together. The applicant's own figures show up to 2,250 people leaving Secret Cinema and the Troubadour together in a single half hour, using the same route. The issue is not whether a Troubadour venue can operate somewhere. It is whether this specific location has enough suitable space, safe routes and enforceable controls for the combined crowds. On enforceable controls, the Troubadour's own operational statement leaves crowd dispersal to each hirer's event safety plan and risk assessment, and treats staggering show times as something that "will be considered", with the two spaces hired out to different operators. That is mitigation by per-event management plan, not the secured, enforceable control this site needs, and the "coordination" the cumulative case relies on is not guaranteed.

Noise also needs to be assessed cumulatively. The riverside is quiet today, and the Troubadour's own acoustic assessment predicts exceedance of the council's noise limit at the neighbouring plot where Secret Cinema would sit. The planning officers' report treated that exceedance as acceptable partly because the worst-affected plot was still undeveloped, the very plot Secret Cinema now proposes to occupy. The applicant's noise document says those exceedance areas would only be assessed after construction, in its own words "in order to not place onerous noise control requirements on the scheme". That is before Secret Cinema's own crowd, music and effects are added.

The path: 1.5 metres, and the crowd it must carry

The applicant's own figures show up to 2,250 people leaving Secret Cinema and the Troubadour together in a single half hour. They would leave onto Olympian Way / the Thames Path, where the marked walking space is about 1.5 metres in places beside a two-way cycle route.

There does not appear to be a proper pedestrian comfort or crowd safety assessment of whether that route can safely and comfortably carry those numbers. On TfL's own Pedestrian Comfort Guidance, that level of crowding would fall into the worst or near-worst comfort bands, depending on what width is assumed.

The Thames Path here is also the Peninsula's P5K route, a five-kilometre walking and running loop for the neighbourhood, conditioned to stay public "in perpetuity". It was planned as public realm and leisure space, not assessed as the main dispersal route for event crowds.

When the path itself went through planning, TfL and amenity groups asked about its capacity. The response was that a detailed assessment was not needed because it was "a leisure route, not the primary commuter route". That matters now, because the same route is being relied on for large venue crowds.

On the applicant's own drawings, the two-way cycle track takes about three metres. The walking strip beside it is about one and a half metres in places, and the route is part of National Cycle Route 1. A crowd would not only affect pedestrians. It would also affect cyclists using the route to pass through. The applicant's Transport Statement describes Olympian Way only as "an identified local cycle route", understating a marked two-way route, part of National Cycle Route 1, that runs directly past the venue entrance.

This is an amenity, not just a route. P5K Phase 2 was a landscaping scheme: a designed public leisure space for walking, running, cycling, sitting and relaxing by the water. A large event crowd bottlenecking onto the path and seating terrace risks turning that public amenity into the venues' forecourt.

It is also part of the Thames Path National Trail, protected by national, regional and local planning policy. If event crowds make it difficult or unsafe to use at peak times, that should be treated as a planning issue in its own right.

The developer says the path "will remain clear" and that a queuing plan will keep crowds off the cycle route. That is not the same as an assessment. The queuing it describes is for arrivals, held on the applicant's own land and justified by "the experience of the applicant" rather than a site-specific assessment. It says nothing about the 2,250 leaving at once, or whether the narrow walking strip can actually hold that crowd. Every visitor is also searched on entry, the Troubadour bag-searching all visitors at its single north gate and Secret Cinema's own information noting a security check on arrival, a per-person check at one path-side entrance that builds a queue at the very pinch point they say will stay clear.

The applicants do set out crowd management: stewards, signage, etiquette reminders, coordination with the Safety Advisory Group and London Underground, and security positioned "to avoid creating a bottleneck or blockage of the public highway" (their own words, conceding the risk). But managing behaviour is not the same as creating capacity: stewarding and signage can shape how a crowd moves, they cannot widen a 1.5-metre path or add trains and buses. These are operational promises for after approval, not a pre-decision assessment that the route and network can take the combined surge, and they are not secured by condition.

The applicant also describes the same route differently in other material. For the residential block now rising to the south, Olympian Way is described as "a public footpath" and "the pedestrian only pathway". Visitor guides promote this riverside as a place for "a leisurely stroll", "a quiet spot", "quiet time to yourself", traffic-free running and cycling, and "tranquil, green spaces". That is the everyday public character a large event crowd would affect.

The applicant calls the 2,250-person peak a worst case and says it is unlikely because the two operators will coordinate show times. But that coordination does not appear to be secured by condition. The two venues share the same developer and the same narrow corridor, and even the applicant's own worst case has not been properly tested against the space available.

Why the lower path does not solve it

There is a lower riverside path, but along the venue frontage it is a separate level, divided from the upper path by seating, loungers and planting, and crossed only at two staircases. It cannot simply be treated as overflow space for a large crowd leaving the venues.

Even if a more generous width than the marked 1.5-metre walking strip is assumed, the crowding still appears to fall into a very uncomfortable band under TfL guidance. The cycle lane is not crowd space, and the applicant does not appear to have assessed this properly.

The route still comes back to Olympian Way

Some material refers to movement through the Design District, but the route to and from the venue still depends on Olympian Way. That is the narrow corridor beside the Thames Path and National Cycle Route 1, so it still needs a proper pedestrian comfort and crowd safety assessment.

The central question is still the same: can up to 2,250 people leave the two venues in a single half hour using Olympian Way / the Thames Path, without blocking the pedestrian route, the cycle route, the lower riverside amenity or the public spaces residents use day to day?

The riverside route was delivered as the P5K Phase 2 route through reserved matters and related applications 16/4183/R, 17/3349/R and 18/0876/F. It was conditioned for pedestrians and cyclists "in perpetuity". In those files, TfL and amenity groups asked about usage and capacity. The response was that "no assessment was deemed necessary" because the route was "a leisure route, not the primary commuter route".

Secret Cinema's own Pirates of the Caribbean visitor map directs ticket-holders to walk from North Greenwich Station to the venue. The applicant's Design and Access Statement gives access "solely via Olympian Way". The route therefore relies on Olympian Way / the Thames Path, whether visitors are described as approaching via the Design District or otherwise.

Pedestrian comfort calculation: TfL's Pedestrian Comfort Guidance for London uses people per minute per metre. For a residential street, PCL D is "very uncomfortable" and PCL E is the worst band; D and E are not acceptable at peak. Applying the applicant's egress figure of about 2,250 people in half an hour, about 75 people per minute, gives about 50 people per minute per metre across a 1.5-metre footway, which is PCL E. Even using wider assumptions gives uncomfortable results: about 29 people per minute per metre at 2.6 metres, PCL D, and about 25 at 3 metres, still worse than the C+ peak floor. The segregated cycle lane has not been counted as footway.

The station and streets are already under pressure

The same station, buses, taxis and local streets already carry major crowds from The O2 and Magazine London. The concern is that no realistic cumulative assessment has tested a busy day across all of these venues together.

After many O2 events, North Greenwich station and the bus station become very crowded for a significant period. Visitors regularly film and post queues and crowding after arena events.

That is the arena on its own, with capacity of around 20,000 people. It is not the only venue on the Peninsula, and the transport infrastructure is already under strain:

TfL describes North Greenwich as the fourth busiest bus station in London and says it "no longer meets capacity requirements". The new bus station intended to address this has not been built. The developer has also applied to push its delivery trigger from 3,000 to 6,000 occupied homes, under S106 variation 24/4133/I106, which has not yet been determined. Event crowds from these venues do not count toward either housing trigger.

Magazine London, also on the Peninsula, holds up to 10,000 people, and its own visitor advice tells people to arrive by Tube and bus. The applications do not appear to model a realistic peak case where The O2, Magazine London, Secret Cinema and the Troubadour all place pressure on the same station, bus interchange, taxis, drop-off points and local streets.

The O2 is also a year-round destination, not just an arena used on event nights. Its outlet, cinema and restaurants create daily visitor numbers, with The O2 as a whole recording over 10 million visitors in 2024. Those visitors already rely heavily on the same Tube and bus interchange.

The applicant argues the show times fall "outside" the typical O2 event timings, so the crowds will not coincide. But on its own figures, Secret Cinema's evening audience leaves between about 9.30pm and 11.30pm, overlapping the O2's typical 10.30pm finish and its post-show exodus, while the Troubadour stays open to midnight or 1am. On an O2 event night, all three would be dispersing across the same late-evening window onto the same station and streets, the cumulative case the applications do not test together.

Even the applicant's own "Cumulative Assessment" covers only Secret Cinema and the Troubadour, and on assumptions, since the Troubadour supplied no arrival or departure data. It does not add The O2 or Magazine London as concurrent loads; instead it argues the venues are "considerably lower than" the O2's capacity, which is a benchmark, not a combined assessment of a busy night across all of them.

There are also existing issues with cars, taxis, private hire and pick-up/drop-off activity on East Parkside, Edmund Halley Way and nearby residential streets. Some visitors already park around the retail park and walk in through the estate. The developer has tried to move pick-ups and drop-offs off the residential streets with a pick-up car park on Millennium Way, but has also acknowledged to residents that East Parkside remains an issue.

Further measures, including taxi geo-fencing and a possible physical barrier, are still unfinished or under consideration. Geo-fencing only reaches app-based taxis, not private cars or family pick-ups. If the venues are approved, pick-up/drop-off, taxi, parking and local street impacts should be assessed and secured by enforceable conditions before opening, not left to informal management after the venues are operating.

The masterplan: this is housing land

These plots were planned for homes. The Peninsula's masterplan placed major destination uses closer to the station and The O2, while the Lower Riverside plots were zoned for housing.

In planning terms, a theatre or cinema of this kind is a "Sui Generis" use: destination entertainment, not a library, museum or community hall. The council's own description of Secret Cinema, 26/1247/F, describes the use as "Sui Generis". The applicant's own planning assessment goes further: against the policy test for new social infrastructure provision (accessible, affordable community services), it answers "not relevant", describing the scheme only as a "public facing use". So even the applicant does not present this, in planning terms, as social or community infrastructure, despite the "community benefit" framing in its public messaging.

Under the 2015 masterplan, the riverside plots, including 20.01 and 20.02 where the venues would go, were zoned for C3 housing. In the 2022 Development Specification, the Peninsula's theatre allocation was placed at the Transport Interchange, by The O2 and the Design District. The riverside plots where these venues would go were not included in that theatre allocation.

That distinction matters. A site beside the Tube, bus interchange and The O2 has better access to transport and more space for people to gather, queue and leave. A Lower Riverside site has homes nearby, a narrower public route, and a different public-realm role.

The Mayor of London's cultural infrastructure guidance also points to the need for venues to have suitable access, servicing, queuing and dispersal space, and recognises that queueing and crowd noise can affect neighbours. Those issues need to be tested against this specific site, not assumed from the general benefit of culture.

The point is not that the Peninsula can never have venues. Knight Dragon previously promoted theatre, cinema and performance uses at Peninsula Place, over the North Greenwich transport interchange, which it described as "the heart of it all". That is a different location from Lower Riverside, outside homes and opening onto Olympian Way / the Thames Path.

The temporary label also matters. These are not weekend pop-ups. The applications could allow commercial venues to operate here for up to ten years. A ten-year "meanwhile" use can still create long-term pressure on paths, streets, homes and services, even if it is not a permanent permission.

There is also a mismatch between the planning label and the public marketing. The planning applications rely on temporary permission, while Secret Cinema has been described as a "new permanent home", "new long-term home" and "flagship home", and Troubadour has been described to investors as "semi-permanent". Residents and councillors should be clear what is actually being approved: a temporary permission that could still last up to a decade.

The planning issues in brief

The issue is not whether Greenwich Peninsula can have culture, theatre or events. The issue is whether this specific site has been properly assessed before a decision is made.

The corridor does not appear to have been properly assessed.The applicant's Transport Statement models up to 2,250 people leaving Secret Cinema and the Troubadour in a single half hour, routed onto Olympian Way and the Thames Path. But it does not appear to include a pedestrian comfort assessment of that corridor, where the marked walking strip is about 1.5 metres beside a two-way cycle route. The Planning Statement says the path will "remain clear / unaffected", but that is an assurance, not an assessment.

This is housing land, with no venue allocation.Plot 20 is zoned residential under the operative 2015 masterplan. The Peninsula's only theatre allocation in the 2022 Development Specification is by the Transport Interchange, and expressly excludes this plot. Instead of amending the masterplan, the venues are being brought forward as separate temporary "meanwhile" permissions for up to ten years, even while Secret Cinema is marketed as a "new permanent home" and the Troubadour is marketed to investors as "semi-permanent".

The venues have been assessed separately.Two adjacent crowd-generating venues, from the same master developer, have been applied for separately. The concern is that neither application properly tests the other, or the realistic cumulative case with The O2, Magazine London, North Greenwich station, buses, taxis, drop-offs and local streets.

The noise case needs cumulative testing.Secret Cinema's noise survey was taken on a night with "no events at The O2", against a 43 to 45 dB baseline. The realistic worst case, both venues turning out while The O2 also empties, does not appear to have been modelled. The Troubadour's own acoustic assessment already predicts theatre noise exceeding the adopted limit at the Secret Cinema plot.

The supporting infrastructure is already under pressure.TfL's own assessment calls North Greenwich the fourth busiest bus station in London and says it "no longer meets capacity requirements". The replacement bus station has not been built. The developer has also applied to push its delivery trigger back from 3,000 to 6,000 occupied homes under S106 variation 24/4133/I106, which has not yet been determined. Event crowds generated by these venues do not count toward either dwelling trigger.

The path is a safeguarded National Trail.Olympian Way here forms part of the Thames Path National Trail. The council's adopted Core Strategy safeguards the existing riverside and Thames paths; London Plan Policy SI 16 requires public access along the waterway to be protected; and the NPPF requires development to protect and enhance public rights of way and access, including National Trails. Sending up to 2,250 people onto this route at peak, without a pedestrian comfort assessment, should be assessed against those policies before any decision is made. National Trail status is material through these policies, not a separate statutory veto.

These are requests for proper assessment before a decision, not objections to culture or theatre.
